## Problem

- Textiles account for the majority of in home air pollution
- Forever plastics and chemicals 
- Skin absorption especially clothing, bedding, and towels
- High friction areas, heat, and sweat (athletic wear, couches, bedding)
- Groin and armpits most at risk
- "natural" products like 100% cotton products isn't enough, they can still shed contaminants for years
- Antimicrobial finishes damage your skin microbiome
- Synthetics trap moisture and breed fungi/bacteria
- Contaminants don't just wash out

## Goal


Our goal is to to minimize the amount of chems and plastics coming into our lungs, our mouths, and through our skin. 

We want pristine materials that our body can either:
1. breakdown 
2. pass through

In an ideal world that would mean zero sythetic textiles.

## Gap

Its not feasible to test yuor home products yourselves, the cost of doing so is more than most of your home goods. Instead we need to look to products that have already been tested and assume the worst about any products we already have.

Then we can focus on the areas we spend the most time touching such as bedding and clothing. 

Minimum:
- OEKO-TEX Standard 100** — tests finished fabric for ~1,000 harmful substances. Most accessible mainstream certification.
- **GOTS (Global Organic Textile Standard)** — organic fiber + chemical processing limits. Strict.

Optimal:
- **MADE SAFE** — broader safety certification across many product categories.

---

## System

The ideal system is remarkably simple:
- if your textile doesn't have an OEKO-TEX cert or better eliminate it
- if you are unsure, eliminate it
- replace with MADE SAFE or OEKO-TEX + GOTS certified textiles

Now, this is big project. The home is almost entirely filled with synthetics. So, here is your priority stack. Start with your bedroom, then your closet, then the rest of the house in order of where you spend the most time.
